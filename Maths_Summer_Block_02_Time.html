Week 1
🕒 O'clock
An o'clock time means 0 minutes past the hour.
The minute hand always points straight up to 12.
Sentence stem: "The long hand is on 12, and the short hand is on 3. The time is 3 o'clock."
Week 1
🕢 Half past
Half past = 30 minutes past the hour. The minute hand has travelled halfway around the clock — so it points straight down to 6.
⚠️ The hour hand is now halfway between two numbers!
Common mistake: at half past 4, the short hand is not pointing at 4 — it has crept halfway to 5. If your hand still points exactly at 4, your clock is wrong!
Week 1
🕒 Quarter past
Quarter past = 15 minutes past the hour. The minute hand has gone ¼ of the way around — it points to 3.
🔗 Link to fractions: a clock face has 4 quarters, just like a pizza! Quarter past = the first quarter (12 → 3) is finished.
Week 1 · Trickiest
🕘 Quarter to
Quarter to = there are 15 minutes left until the next hour. The minute hand points to 9.
⚠️ The hour hand is nearly at the next number — almost there!
Trap: at quarter to 5, the short hand is almost at the 5 — but the time is "quarter to 5" because the minute hand still has to travel up to 12. We name the hour we are going to, not the one we are leaving!

Week 1 · 12-Hour Clock
🌅 a.m.  &  p.m. 🌙
A clock face only has 12 numbers, but a day has 24 hours. The hour hand goes round twice each day. So we use a.m. and p.m. to know which trip we're on.
a.m.
"ante meridiem" = before midday
From 12:00 midnight until 12:00 noon.
Morning times: 7:00 a.m. is breakfast.
p.m.
"post meridiem" = after midday
From 12:00 noon until 12:00 midnight.
Afternoon & evening: 7:00 p.m. is dinner.
Memory hook: a.m. = "at morning". p.m. = "past midday". 12:00 noon and 12:00 midnight are special — neither a.m. nor p.m.!

📋 Cheat Sheet — Everything You Need
One page. Print it. Stick it on the fridge.

🕒 The Hands

Short, fat hand = hour. Long, thin hand = minutes. The hour hand drifts between numbers as the minutes pass.

🟦 The Big Four

O'clock: minute hand on 12.
Quarter past: on 3.
Half past: on 6.
Quarter to: on 9.

🔢 Counting in 5s

Each number on the clock = 5 minutes. So 3 → 15 mins, 6 → 30 mins, 9 → 45 mins, 12 → 0 (or 60).

↔️ Past or To?

:01 to :30 → say "past" the current hour.
:31 to :59 → say "to" the next hour. (Subtract from 60.)

⌚ Digital Format

HH : MM — hour, then minutes.
Quarter to 5 = 4:45 (still hour 4!).
Quarter past 5 = 5:15.

📐 Time Units

60 secs = 1 min · 60 mins = 1 hour · 24 hrs = 1 day · 7 days = 1 week · 12 months = 1 year · 365 days = 1 year (366 leap).

📅 Days & Months

Mon, Tue, Wed, Thu, Fri, Sat, Sun.
Jan, Feb (28/29), Mar, Apr (30), May, Jun (30), Jul, Aug, Sep (30), Oct, Nov (30), Dec.

🌅 a.m. / p.m.

a.m. = 12 midnight → 12 noon (morning).
p.m. = 12 noon → 12 midnight (afternoon, evening, night).

⏳ Find Duration

Jump from start → next o'clock → end. Count the chunks. Or use a number line / bar model.

🍂 Seasons

Spring (Mar–May) · Summer (Jun–Aug) · Autumn (Sep–Nov) · Winter (Dec–Feb).

🔁 Conversions

1 hr = 60 min · ½ hr = 30 min · ¼ hr = 15 min · 90 min = 1 hr 30 min · 75 min = 1 hr 15 min.

⚠️ Top Mistakes

1. Hour hand stuck on a number at half past.
2. "Quarter to 5" → digital is 4:45 not 5:45.
3. Forgetting to cross the hour at :00.

Vocabulary
📖 Glossary — Time Vocabulary
Every word you'll meet in this block.
Analog clock
A clock with a round face and two hands that move around. Sometimes called a "round clock".
Digital clock
A clock that shows the time as numbers, like 3:15. Found on phones, ovens, microwaves.
Hour hand
The short, fat hand on an analog clock. Points to the current hour. Drifts slowly between numbers.
Minute hand
The long, thin hand on an analog clock. Goes once around the clock every hour.
Second hand
A very thin, fast-moving hand on some clocks. Goes once around every minute (60 seconds).
O'clock
An exact hour, with no extra minutes. The minute hand points straight up to 12.
Half past
30 minutes past the hour. The minute hand points to 6, the hour hand sits halfway between two numbers.
Quarter past
15 minutes past the hour. The minute hand points to 3.
Quarter to
15 minutes before the next hour. The minute hand points to 9. The hour hand is almost at the next number.
Duration
How long something lasts. Found by counting from the start time to the end time.
a.m.
"Ante meridiem" — before midday. Times from midnight (12:00) to noon (12:00).
p.m.
"Post meridiem" — after midday. Times from noon (12:00) to midnight (12:00).
Noon / midday
12:00 in the middle of the day. The end of "morning" and start of "afternoon".
Midnight
12:00 at the very middle of the night. The end of one day and start of the next.
Second
The smallest unit of time we use. 60 seconds = 1 minute.
Minute
60 seconds. The minute hand moves one tick every minute. 60 minutes = 1 hour.
Hour
60 minutes. The hour hand moves from one number to the next every hour.
Day
24 hours. The hour hand goes round the clock twice in a day.
Week
7 days. Monday through Sunday.
Month
A part of the year. Most months have 30 or 31 days; February has 28 (or 29 in a leap year).
Year
12 months, or 365 days (366 in a leap year).
Leap year
A special year with an extra day (29 February). Happens every 4 years.
Season
One of the four parts of the year — Spring, Summer, Autumn, Winter.
Calendar
A chart showing the days, weeks and months of a year.
Roman numeral
An old way to write numbers using letters: I, II, III, IIII (or IV), V, VI, VII, VIII, IX, X, XI, XII.

🦁 Genius · 24-Hour Clock
🕓 The 24-Hour Clock
Trains, planes and the army don't use a.m./p.m. — they use the 24-hour clock. After 12:00 noon they keep counting: 13, 14, 15… up to 23:59. Then it rolls over to 00:00 (midnight).

🦁 Genius · Across Midnight
🌙 Durations Across Midnight
When a duration crosses midnight, the end time is on a different day. Split the journey at midnight and add the two halves.
Strategy: jump 1 — start time → 12:00 midnight. Jump 2 — midnight → end time. Add the two. Example: 11:00 pm → 6:00 am = 1 hr + 6 hrs = 7 hours.
🦁 Genius · Clock Angles
📐 Angles Between the Hands
A full circle = 360°. The clock has 12 numbers, so each "hour gap" is 360 ÷ 12 = 30°. The minute hand moves 6° per minute; the hour hand a tiny 0.5° per minute.
Quick formula (Y3+): angle = | (30 × hour) − (5.5 × minute) |. At 3:00 the answer is 30×3 − 0 = 90° (a right angle!).
🦁 Genius · Hand Logic
🤝 When Do the Hands Overlap?
Both hands point the same way at 12:00. The minute hand laps the hour hand again about every 1 hour 5 minutes — so over 12 hours, they overlap exactly 11 times (not 12 — the lapping is what makes it 11).
Why 11, not 12? In 12 hours the hour hand goes round once and the minute hand goes round 12 times — so the minute hand "catches up" 12 − 1 = 11 times. Beautiful, isn't it?
🦁 Genius · Right Angles
📐 When Are the Hands at 90°?
A right angle = 90°. The clearest example is 3:00 and 9:00 — the hands form a perfect L.
Mind-bender: in 12 hours, the hands form a right angle 22 times — about once every 32 minutes and 43 seconds. Because the hour hand keeps moving, the cycle fits 22 times into 12 hours (not 24!).

🕵️ Multi-Clock Puzzles

🦁 Genius · Open Investigation
🔬 Open Investigations
No multiple choice. No "right answer". Just thinking. Type your idea, then peek at a model answer.

1. Why 60 minutes — not 100?

Most things in maths are based on 10s (we have 10 fingers, money goes 1p, 10p, £1, £10…). But an hour has 60 minutes, not 100. Why? Make a guess.
Show one possible answer
The Babylonians (3,000+ years ago) loved the number 60 because it splits cleanly so many ways: 60 ÷ 2 = 30, ÷ 3 = 20, ÷ 4 = 15, ÷ 5 = 12, ÷ 6 = 10. That meant they could split an hour into halves, thirds, quarters, fifths and sixths — all whole numbers. 100 only splits cleanly into 2, 4, 5, 10. We kept the 60 because it's just so much more useful for cutting time into equal parts.

2. Design a 10-hour clock.

Imagine a planet where the day has only 10 hours instead of 24. How long would each "hour" be on Earth? How many minutes in one of these new hours? Sketch what the clock face might look like in your head.
Show one possible answer
A real Earth day = 24 hours = 1,440 minutes. Splitting that into 10 new hours gives 144 minutes per new hour — more than twice as long as ours! The clock face would have only 10 numbers around it (instead of 12), spaced 36° apart instead of 30°. France actually tried "decimal time" during the Revolution in 1793 — they had 10 hours per day, 100 minutes per hour, 100 seconds per minute. It lasted about a year before everyone gave up and went back to the 24-hour day.

3. Could you tell the time on a clock with no numbers?

Some clocks are pure decoration — just hands, no numbers. Could you still read them? What features of the clock face would you need?
Show one possible answer
Yes, as long as the clock has 12 evenly-spaced markers (ticks, dots, anything) and you know which one is "12". Our brains learn to use the position of the hands more than the numbers themselves. Many luxury watches have only 4 marks (12, 3, 6, 9), and people read them just fine. The numbers are a helpful guide for beginners — but the geometry of the circle is what really tells the time.

4. Why does the hour hand move at all when no hour has passed?

When it's 3:30, the hour hand has drifted halfway between 3 and 4 — even though it's still "3 something". Why don't clockmakers just leave the hour hand stuck on 3 until exactly 4:00? Would that be better or worse? Argue for either side.
Show one possible answer
A "jumping" hour hand would be easier to read for beginners — but it would lose precision. The smooth drift is actually a feature: at a glance you can tell whether it's early in the hour (hand near 3) or late (hand near 4) without even looking at the minute hand. It's a built-in "fraction-of-the-hour" indicator. Mechanically, smooth drift is also what gears naturally produce — to make it jump you'd need extra ratchets, which would cost more and be less reliable.
