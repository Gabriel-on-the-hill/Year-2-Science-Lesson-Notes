Step 1 of 7 · Tally charts
✏️ How tally marks work
A tally mark is a single line. Every 5th mark goes across the other four to make a gate. This makes counting fast — just count in 5s!
To show a number as tally marks, draw groups of 5 and then single lines.

Step 2 · Tables
📋 Same data, two views
A table just shows the totals. A tally chart shows how you collected the data. Same numbers — different jobs.

    Step 3 · Block diagrams
    🧱 From cubes to blocks
    Each block stands for one. Stack them in equal-sized columns. The taller the column, the more popular the category.

    Step 4 · Pictograms (1 to 1)
    🖼️ One picture = one thing
    A pictogram uses pictures instead of bars. The key tells you what one picture means. Use the same picture every time!
    🔑 Key: ⚽ = 1 goal scored
    If 1 ⚽ = 1 goal and Mo scored 4, I draw 4 footballs in Mo's row.

      Bonus skill · Reading into data
      📖 Turn a story into a tally chart
      Read the paragraph. Highlight the numbers in your head. Then fill in the totals.
      James asked everyone in his class what their favourite sport was. Ten people chose football and three people chose rugby. Bella and Katy chose dance and eight people said tennis. James' favourite sport is cricket.
      Step 6 · Bigger keys (2, 5, 10)
      ⚖️ Why a bigger key helps
      If you had to draw 30 children with 1 picture each, you'd run out of room! A key of 10 means 1 picture = 10 children. Same data — much tidier.
      🔑 Key: 🟣 = 1 child (messy!)
      🔑 Key: 🟣 = 10 children (tidy!)
      A half-picture means half of the key. So if 🟣 = 10, then 🟣½ = 5.

      Step 7 · Read bigger keys (2, 5, 10)
      🐦 Counting with the key
      Count the pictures, then multiply by the key. Half a picture means half the key.
      🔑 Key: 🟦 = 5 birds
      Sparrow: 3 pictures × 5 = 15 birds.
      Robin: 1 picture × 5 = 5 birds.
      Difference: 15 − 5 = 10 birds.

      🎒 Take-home challenge

      🟢 Mild · 5 minutes
      📋 Tally hunt. Sit by a window or in a quiet room and tally every sound you hear for 5 minutes (cars, dogs, voices, doors). Use a gate of 5 for every 5th mark.
      🟡 Medium · 10 minutes
      📊 Make a table of your bookshelf. Sort books into 3–4 categories (story, fact, comic, picture-book). Tally each, then write a table with just the totals.
      🟣 Spicy · 15 minutes
      🖼️ Pictogram with a key of 2. Use the bookshelf data. Choose a symbol that's easy to halve. Draw the pictogram. Then write 2 questions about your own data ("How many more story books than comics?").
      💬 Talk to a grown-up: "When would a tally chart be better than a table? When would a table be better?"
      Grown-up answer key
      Tally vs table: tally is best when you're still collecting (e.g. counting cars going past — you can't see them all at once). A table is best when you want to read the total quickly.
      Half-able symbols: circles ●, squares ■, hearts ♥ — easy to split. Avoid stars ★ or animals — hard to draw a clean half.
      Good questions to ask about own data: "Which is the most popular?", "How many altogether?", "How many more X than Y?", "What is the difference between X and Y?"
